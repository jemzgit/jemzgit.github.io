Derivative

Geometrically, the derivative $g^{\prime}$ gives the slope of the secant line through the point $(x, f(x))$.

Let $f:[a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued function. There are two identical ways to define the derivative of $f$ at point $x \in [a,b]$:

$$ f^{\prime} = \lim_{x \to h} \dfrac{f(x) - f(h)}{x-h} $$ $$ f^{\prime} = \lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} $$

We say that $f$ is differentable at $x$.

There are several notations to indicate the derivative of a function $f$:

  • The Lagrange notation $f^{\prime}(x)$: The most widely used notation of derivatives in mathematics so $f^{\prime} (x) $ is the derivative at $x$.
  • The Leibniz notation $\dfrac{df(x)}{dx}$: This notation is to indicate the limit of difference quotient. Note that $\dfrac{df(x)}{dx}$ is NOT a fraction and $df(x)$ and $dx$ are NOT numbers. We think of $d/dx$ as the differentiation operator; For example if $f(x) = x^2$, then $d/dx(x^2) = 2x$. Furthermore, we call $dx$ the differential.
    • $$ \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \dfrac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \dfrac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{df(x)}{dx} $$
  • The Newton dot notation $\dot{f}(x)$
  • The Euler notation $D_x f(x)$.

In some textbooks, authors set $y=f(x)$ and call $x$ the independent variable and call $y$ the dependent variable ($y$ value depends on $x$)

Theorem: If $f: A \to \mathbb{R}$ is differentiable at a point $ x \in A$, then $f$ is continuous at $x$ as well. In other words, if $f$ is not continuous at $x$, then $f$ is not differentiable at $x$.

Hence differentiability at a point implies continuity, but continuity does not necessarily imply differntiability.

Theorem. Algebraic Differentiability Theorem. Let $f$ and $g$ be two functions defined on interval $[a,b]$ that are differentiable at a point $x \in [a,b]$. Then,

$f^{\prime}(k) = 0$ for some $k \in \mathbb{R}$.

$(kf)^{\prime}(x) = kf^{\prime}(x)$ for all $k$ in $\mathbb{R}$.

$(f + g)^{\prime}(x) = f^{\prime}(x) + g^{\prime}(x)$.

(Product Rule). $(fg)^{\prime}(x) = f^{\prime}(x)g(x) + f(x)g^{\prime}(x)$.

(Quotient Rule). $(\dfrac{f}{g})^{\prime}(x) = \dfrac{g(x)f^{\prime}(x) - g^{\prime}(x)f(x)}{g^{2}(x)}$

(Chain Rule). Let $f: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ and $g:[t,s] \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfy $f([a,b]) \subseteq [t,s]$ so that the composition $g \circ f$ is defined. If $f$ is differentiable at $x \in [a,b]$ and if $g$ is differentiable at $f(x) \in [t,s]$, then $g \circ f$ is differentiable at $c$ with $(g \circ f)^{\prime} = g^{\prime}(f(x)) \cdot f(x)$.

Using the Leibniz notation, The composite function $g \circ f$ becomes:

$$\dfrac{df}{dx} = \dfrac{df}{dg} \dfrac{dg}{dx}$$

For example, to calculate $f(x) = (4x^2 - 2x + 5)^3$, let $g(x)=4x^2 - 2x + 5$ and let $f(x)=g(x)^3$. then,

$$\dfrac{df}{dx} = \dfrac{df}{dg} \dfrac{dg}{dx} = 3(g(x))^2 (8x -2) = 3(4x^2 - 2x + 5)^2 (8x-2)$$

(Inverse-function Rule). Let $f^{-1}$ be the inverse function of $f$ such that $f$ has a non-zero derivative at $x$, then $f^{-1}$ has a derivative at $y = f(x)$ and $(f^{-1})^{\prime}(y) = \dfrac{1}{f^{\prime}(x)}$

(Power Rule). $f^{\prime}(x^m) = m x^{m-1}$.

Note that if instead of $x$ was a function $g(x)$, we can use chain rule for power rule:

$f^{\prime}(g(x)^m) = m(g(x))^{m-1} g^{\prime}(x)$.

Or in Leibniz notation, if we set $u=g(x)$ then $d(u^m) = mu^{m-1}du$

$f^{\prime}(e^{x}) = e^x$.

$f^{\prime}(a^x) = (\log_e a)(a^x) = (\ln a)(a^x)$.

$f^{\prime}(\log_a x) = \dfrac{1}{x \log a}$, $a \neq 1$.

Trig Functions Derivatives

Trig Functions derivatives
$f^{\prime}(\sin(x)) = \cos(x) $
$f^{\prime}(\cos(x)) = -\sin(x)$
$f^{\prime}(\tan(x)) = \sec^{2}(x)$
$f^{\prime}(\cot(x)) = -\csc^{2}(x)$
$f^{\prime}(\sec(x)) = \sec(x)\tan(x)$
$f^{\prime}(\csc(x)) = \csc(x)\cot(x)$

Note that the derivatives of cofunctions (e.g. $\cos$, $\cot$, $\csc$) have a negative sign. The derivate of cofunction of a function is the cofunction of the derivates of that function. For example, the derivative of $\cot(x)$ is the cofunctions of derivatives of $\tan(x)$ (plus the negaitve sign).

Inverse Trig Functions Derivatives

Note that the derivative of cofunction has a negative sign.

Inverse Trig Function

$f^{\prime}(\arccos(x)) = \dfrac{-1}{\sqrt{(1-x^2)}}$
$f^{\prime}(\arctan(x)) = \dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$

Implicit Differentiation

Implicit differentiation is special case of chain rule:

$$x^2 y^5 + y = 2$$ $$(x^2 \cdot 5y^4 y^{\prime} + 2xy^5) + y^{\prime} = 0$$ $$y^{\prime}(5x^{2}y^{4} + 1) = -2xy^5$$ $$y^{\prime}= - \dfrac{2xy^{5}}{5x^2 y^4 + 1}$$

Mean Value Theorem

Let $f:X \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued function from metric space $X$. We define the local maximum at a point $a \in X$ if there exists $\delta >0$ such that $f(b) \leq f(a)$ for all $b \in X$ with $d(a,b) < \delta$. Local minimum is defined similarly. The following theorem is crucial in theory of differentiation:

(Interior Extremum Theorem) . Let $f:[a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be function. If $f$ has a local maximum (or local minimum) at a point $x \in (a,b)$ and if $f^{\prime}(x)$ exists, then $f^{\prime}(x)= 0$.

(Rolle Theorem). Let $f:[a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$. If $f(a) = f(b)$, then there exists a point $x \in (a,b)$ where $f^{\prime}(x) = 0$.

(Mean Value Theorem). If $f:[a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, then there exists a point $x \in (a,b)$ such that:

$$ f^{\prime}(x) = \dfrac{f(b) - f(a)}{b-a}$$

Note that if $f(a) = f(b)$, then mean value theorem becomes the Rolles theorem.

(Generalized Mean Value Theorem). If $f$ and $g$ are continuous on the closed interval $[a,b]$ and differentable on the open interval $(a,b)$, then there exists a point $x \in (a,b)$ such that:

$$[f(b) - f(a)]g^{\prime}(x) = [g(b)-g(a)]f^{\prime}(x)$$

If $g^{\prime}$ is never zero on $(a,b)$, then:

$$\dfrac{f^{\prime}(x)}{g^{\prime}(x)} = \dfrac{f(b) - f(a)}{g(b)-g(a)}$$

(Darboux Theorem) If $f$ is differentiable on $[a,b]$ and suppose that $\alpha$ satisfies $f^{\prime}(a) < \alpha < f^{\prime}(b)$ ( or $f^{\prime}(a) > \alpha > f^{\prime}(b)$), then there exists a point $c \in (a,b)$ such that $f^{\prime}(c)= \alpha$.

Graphing Using Derivatives

The first and second derivative of a function can be very helpful for studing the behavior of a function:

Properties of The First Derivative: Suppose $f$ is differentiable on interval $(a,b)$.

(a) If $f^{\prime} \geq 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is monotically increasing and the slope is positive.

(b) If $f^{\prime} = 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is a constant function and the slope is zero (has a horizontal tanget line). In this case, we call $x$ a critical or stationary point of $f$ which often signifies a turning point.

(c) If $f^{\prime} \leq 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is monotically decreasing and the slope is negative.

Properties of The Second Derivative: Suppose $f$ has a well-defined second derivative on interval $(a,b)$.

(a) If $f^{\prime \prime}(x) >0$, then the curve concave up or convex.

(b) If $f^{\prime \prime}(x) <0$, then the curve concave down or concave.

(c) Suppose $f^{\prime \prime}$ is continuous at $x$. If the point $x$ is an inflection point (i.e. concavity changes), then $f^{\prime \prime}(x) =0$. Also an inflection point can happen at a point $x$ where $f^{\prime \prime}$ is undefined.

Min/Max

If $f^{\prime}(x) = 0$ and $f^{\prime \prime}(x) > 0$, then $f(x)$ is a local minimum.

If $f^{\prime}(x) = 0$ and $f^{\prime \prime}(x) < 0$, then $f(x)$ is a local maximum.

If $f$ is defined on a closed interval $[a,b]$, then the Extreme Value Theorem of Continuity guarntees that $f$ will actually attain an absolute minimum and an absolute maximum. There possible locations of absolute extrema are at $f^{\prime}(x)=0$, $f^{\prime}(x)$ is undefined, or at the endpoints $a$ and $b$.

L'Hospital's Rule Suppose $f$ and $g$ are continuous and differentiable functions on an interval $(a,b)$ containing $c$, and $g(x) \neq 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$. Suppose that $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = 0$ and $\lim_{x \to c} g(x) = 0$ (or only if $\lim_{x \to c} g(x) = \infty$), then:

$$\lim_{x \to c} \dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)} = L = \lim_{x \to c} \dfrac{f^{\prime}(x)}{g^{\prime}(x)}$$

L'Hospital's Rule is a powerful tool for evaluating limits of functions
