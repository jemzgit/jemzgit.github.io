Trigonometry

There are six basic trigonometric functions: $\sin(\theta)$, $\cos(\theta)$, $\tan(\theta)$, $\csc(\theta)$, $\sec(\theta)$, $\cot(\theta)$. They are functions of angles.

For many purposes, it is convenient to work with circle of radius 1: unit circle.

Degrees and Radians

Angles can be mesaured in degrees and radians. The degree of the angle $\theta$ is determined by the formula $\dfrac{Area\; of\; secter}{Area\; of\; circle} = \dfrac{\theta}{360}$. For example, to calculate the degree of a right angle, we notice that the area of the unit circle is $\pi$ and the area of the secter is $\dfrac{\pi}{4}$. Hence $\dfrac{\pi \mathbin{/} 4}{\pi} = \dfrac{\theta}{360}$. After some simplification, $\theta = 90$ degrees which is denoted by $90^{\circ}$. The same formula can be used to calculate the area of a sector.

To visualize radians, take the radius of any circle and stretch it across the circumference of the circle. The angle that it subtends equals to one radian. In fact, 1 radian = $57.3^{\circ}$ degrees. To see this, Recall that circumference of a circle is $2\pi r$ so the circumference of the unit circle is $2\pi$ which subtends an angle of $360^{\circ}$; that is $360$ degrees = $2 \pi$ radians. So 1 degree = $\dfrac{2\pi}{360} = \dfrac{\pi}{180}$ radians. Conversely, 1 radian = $\dfrac{180}{\pi} = 57.3$ degrees.

Note 1: It is very useful to remember the important radians: $30^{\circ} = \dfrac{\pi}{6}$, $45^{\circ} = \dfrac{\pi}{4}$, $60^{\circ} = \dfrac{\pi}{3}$, $90^{\circ} = \dfrac{\pi}{2}$. Now we can mentally calculate other angles in radians. For example, $150^{\circ} = 5(30^{\circ}) = 5(\dfrac{\pi}{6}) = \dfrac{5\pi}{6} $

Trig functions of Acute Angles

There are six basic trigonometric functions: $\sin(\theta)$, $\cos(\theta)$, $\tan(\theta)$ and their reciprocals $\csc(\theta)$, $\sec(\theta)$, $\cot(\theta)$. If $f(x)$ is a function its reciprocal is defined as $\dfrac{1}{f(x)}$. A function multplied by its reciprocals equals 1. For example, $\sin(\theta)\csc(\theta) = 1$. The trigonometric functions of acute angle ($\theta < 90^{\circ}$) is defined as follows:

Trig Function Reciprocal
$\sin(\theta) = \dfrac{a}{c}$ $\csc(\theta) = \dfrac{c}{a}$
$\cos(\theta) = \dfrac{b}{c}$ $\sec(\theta) = \dfrac{c}{b}$
$\tan(\theta) = \dfrac{a}{b}$ $\cot(\theta) = \dfrac{b}{a}$


Note the recpirocal of a trig function is not same as the cofunction of that function. The cofunctions of $\sin$, $\tan$, $\sec$ are $\cos$, $\cot$, and $\csc$.

Rememeber that $co$ prefix can be used to identify the reicprocal of each function.

No need to memorize this table. You can construct it easily:

  • $\sin(\theta)$ is just the sequence $\dfrac{\sqrt{0}}{2},\dfrac{\sqrt{1}}{2}, \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}...$
  • $\cos(\theta)$ is the same sequence in reverse order: $\dfrac{\sqrt{4}}{2},\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}...$
  • $\tan(\theta)$ is just $\sin(\theta) / \cos(\theta)$.
  • $\cot(\theta)$, $\sec(\theta)$, $\csc(\theta)$ are reciprocals of $\tan(\theta)$, $\cos(\theta)$, and $\sin(\theta)$ respectively.

Opposite Angle Indentities

$$ \sin(-\theta) = -\sin(\theta) $$ $$ \cos(-\theta) = \cos(\theta) $$

According to these identities, $f(x)=\sin(x)$ is an odd function that is $f(-x) = -f(x)$. Also $f(x)=\cos(x)$ is an even function that is $f(x) = f(-x)$.

Pythageran Identities

$$ \sin^{2}(\theta) + \cos^{2}(\theta) = 1 $$ $$ 1 + \tan^{2}(\theta) = \sec^{2}(\theta) $$ $$ 1 + \cot^{2} = \csc^{2}(\theta) $$

Addition and Substraction Formulae (Compound-Angle Formulae)

$\sin(\alpha + \beta) = \sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta) + \cos(\alpha)\sin(\beta)$

$\sin(\alpha - \beta) = \sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta) - \cos(\alpha)\sin(\beta)$

$\cos(\alpha + \beta) = \cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta) - \sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta)$

$\cos(\alpha - \beta) = \cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta) + \sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta)$

$\tan(\alpha + \beta) = \dfrac{\tan(\alpha) + \tan(\beta)}{1 - \tan(\alpha)\tan(\beta)}$

$\tan(\alpha - \beta) = \dfrac{\tan(\alpha) - \tan(\beta)}{1 + \tan(\alpha)\tan(\beta)}$

Double Angle Formulae

$$ \sin(2\theta) = 2\sin(\theta)\cos(\theta) $$ $$ \cos(2\theta) = \cos^{2}(\theta) - \sin^{2}(\theta) $$ $$ = 1 - 2\sin^{2}(\theta) $$ $$ = 2\cos^{2}(\theta) - 1 $$ $$ \cos^{2}(\theta) = \dfrac{1 + \cos(2\theta)}{2} $$ $$ \sin^{2}(\theta) = \dfrac{1 - \cos(2\theta)}{2} $$ $$ \tan(2\theta) = \dfrac{2\tan(\theta)}{1 - \tan^{2}(\theta)} $$

Complementary Angle (Reduction) Formulae

$$ \sin(\dfrac{\pi}{2} - \theta) = \cos(\theta) $$ $$ \cos(\dfrac{\pi}{2} - \theta) = \sin(\theta) $$

Half-Angle Formulae

$$ \sin(\dfrac{\theta}{2}) = \pm \sqrt{\dfrac{1-\cos(\theta)}{2}} $$ $$ \cos(\dfrac{\theta}{2}) = \pm \sqrt{\dfrac{1+\cos(\theta)}{2}} $$ $$ \tan(\dfrac{\theta}{2}) = \dfrac{\sin(\theta)}{1+\cos(\theta)} $$

Law of Sines

$$ \dfrac{\sin(A)}{a} = \dfrac{\sin(B)}{b} = \dfrac{\sin(C)}{c} $$

Law of Cosines

$$ c^{2} = a^{2} + b^{2} - 2ab\cos{\theta} $$
